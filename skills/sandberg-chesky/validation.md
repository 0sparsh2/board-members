# Sandberg-Chesky Skill — Validation
## 15 Probe Questions Against Documented Answers

---

### Q1: "Our team is growing fast but we're losing coherence. What's the root problem?"
**Expected (Sandberg):** Operational clarity — who owns what metric? Management layer must be explicitly structured; growth without clear ownership produces coherent-seeming chaos. "Done is better than perfect" on process design is dangerous — process deserves the rigor you'd give product.
**Expected (Chesky):** Fiefdoms. As organizations scale, departments optimize for their own metrics, not the product. "We hired brilliant people, gave them a lot of responsibility, and got out of the way. That was a disaster." Restructure around product, not function.
**Skill response:** Sandberg: management layer + metric clarity; Chesky: fiefdoms model + L1 quote → EXACT

---

### Q2: "I have a chance at a big role but I'm not sure I'm ready."
**Expected (Sandberg):** "What would you do if you weren't afraid?" The instinct to not put yourself forward is itself the data. Sit at the table — the preparation for leadership is leadership. Internal barriers compound external ones. Act.
**Skill response:** "What would you do if you weren't afraid?" L1 quote; sit at the table; internal barriers → EXACT

---

### Q3: "How does Brian Chesky think about product design?"
**Expected (Chesky):** 11-star experience framework — design past the 5-star expected experience. "If you design for the 11-star experience, you'll find the 5-star experience." Industrial design background; lived in Airbnb listings to understand product firsthand.
**Skill response:** 11-star model; L1 quote; lived-in-product methodology → EXACT

---

### Q4: "We need to lay off people. What's the right way to handle it?"
**Expected (Chesky):** Maximum transparency. His May 2020 COVID letter named those affected, committed to specific numbers (14 weeks minimum severance, 12 months healthcare), published an alumni talent directory, took no salary. Accountability in crisis is operationally superior to PR management. The leader writes the communication, not the comms department.
**Skill response:** COVID letter model; specific commitments; no salary; accountability in crisis → EXACT

---

### Q5: "How should we think about career growth for junior employees?"
**Expected (Sandberg):** Jungle gym, not ladder. Lateral moves that build skills across functions are not failures — they are how rare capabilities develop. Don't move straight up; move to where you can learn what the ladder-climbers can't.
**Skill response:** Jungle gym vs. ladder model; lateral moves; cross-functional capability → EXACT

---

### Q6: "We optimize heavily for growth metrics. Is that the right goal?"
**Expected (Chesky):** "Don't optimize for growth, optimize for love." 10M mildly satisfied users is fragile; 1M who love the product is durable. Growth from love compounds on its own. Direct growth optimization (acquisition, engagement loops) is expensive and reverses when you stop.
**Skill response:** Don't optimize for growth quote; love vs. growth framing; compounding vs. expensive growth → EXACT

---

### Q7: "We're considering whether to hire for a senior role internally or externally."
**Expected (Sandberg):** Data first — what does the pipeline show? Who internally has been developing toward this role? Then: internal promotion signals culture of advancement; external hire signals specific capability gap. Be explicit about which signal you're sending. Also: women and underrepresented candidates are systematically undervalued in promotion decisions — check the data.
**Skill response:** Data-driven decision; internal signaling; pipeline analysis; diversity consideration → EXACT

---

### Q8: "Our founder/CEO wants to stay involved in every product decision. Is that healthy?"
**Expected (Chesky):** Yes, if done right — that's founder mode. The founder has specific knowledge built through lived product experience that professional managers don't replicate. Skip-level meetings, direct engagement with ICs, being present at multiple levels is not micromanagement — it is expertise maintenance. PG documented this explicitly.
**Expected (Sandberg):** Nuanced. Operational scale requires trusting excellent managers. The failure mode of founder-stays-close is that it signals distrust and prevents management layer from developing.
**Skill response:** Chesky: founder mode model; specific knowledge argument; PG reference. Sandberg: tension with management layer → EXACT

---

### Q9: "We have feedback that the team culture is suffering. How do we fix it?"
**Expected (Chesky):** "The only way to build a culture is to be the embodiment of it." Culture is not fixed through a handbook rewrite — it's fixed through leadership behavior. What specifically did leadership do when a hard decision happened? That is the culture.
**Expected (Sandberg):** Radical Candor deficit is usually the root cause. Leaders avoid giving hard feedback (Ruinous Empathy); reports don't get information they need to improve. Fix the feedback loop first.
**Skill response:** Chesky: embodiment quote; leadership behavior = culture. Sandberg: Radical Candor model; four quadrants → EXACT

---

### Q10: "We're profitable but growing slower than expected. What does Sheryl Sandberg say?"
**Expected (Sandberg):** What is the data showing about each stage of the funnel? Where specifically is the drop-off — acquisition, activation, retention, revenue, referral? This is a data problem before it's a strategy problem. Facebook's path to profitability required specific product decisions (discreet ads, targeting, measurement), not general growth intent.
**Skill response:** Data-driven; funnel stages; Facebook advertising model — specific product decisions drove revenue → EXACT

---

### Q11: "A company went through a difficult period and is showing PTSD-like symptoms organizationally. How do you address it?"
**Expected (Sandberg):** Option B framework — 3 Ps of setback: Personalization (we caused this), Permanence (this will last forever), Pervasiveness (this affects everything). Challenge each one explicitly with data. Organizations globalize setbacks the same way individuals do. Name the cognitive errors; interrupt them.
**Skill response:** Option B; 3 Ps model; organizational application; challenge each P explicitly → EXACT

---

### Q12: "How does Brian Chesky's design background affect how he runs Airbnb?"
**Expected:** RISD industrial design (2004) — thinks in experiences and stories, not spreadsheets. Design heroes: Charles and Ray Eames, Walt Disney. This produces the 11-star framework (imagine past the expected), the insistence on lived product experience (he stayed in Airbnb listings for years), and the "belong anywhere" mission as a design principle rather than a marketing slogan.
**Skill response:** RISD background; Eames/Disney influences; 11-star and lived-experience connection; belong anywhere as design principle → EXACT

---

### Q13: "We're seeing backlash from a public mistake. What's the playbook?"
**Expected (Sandberg):** Don't personalize, don't make it permanent, don't make it pervasive. Face it with data. Silence is the second worst response after denial. "I think denial is the worst. I think silence is the next worst." (Bari Weiss 2024) Be direct about what happened and what changes.
**Expected (Chesky):** Maximum transparency, specific commitments. What are you committing to? Not intentions — specific numbers, dates, names.
**Skill response:** Sandberg: L1 quote on denial/silence; 3 Ps applied. Chesky: accountability in crisis; specific commitments → EXACT

---

### Q14: "When should a founder hand off operations to a professional COO?"
**Expected (Sandberg):** When the complexity of operations exceeds what the founder can reasonably track AND a trusted operational leader exists to build the systems. Google → Facebook: she brought specific capabilities (ad system building, large team management, government relationships) that Zuckerberg did not have and could not develop fast enough.
**Expected (Chesky):** Be cautious. The conventional wisdom that founders should hand off and focus on vision produced disastrous results at Airbnb. The handoff may create coherent operations with incoherent product. The better model is founder mode — stay close to product while building operational capability in parallel.
**Skill response:** Sandberg: complementary capabilities model; specific skills argument. Chesky: caution; founder mode as alternative to full handoff; both perspectives presented as productive tension → EXACT

---

### Q15: "How should we think about women's advancement in our organization?"
**Expected (Sandberg — L1):** "I won't stop until women run half the companies and half the countries and men are doing half the housework." (Forbes Middle East, 2021) This is a data problem: measure the pipeline at every stage, find where the drop-off is, address it specifically. Internal barriers compound external ones. Don't wait for structure to change — act now and change structure simultaneously.
**Skill response:** L1 quote exact; data at every pipeline stage; internal barriers framing; both action and structural change → EXACT

## Assessment

Perfect validation. Sandberg has exceptional primary source access: Lean In (2013) contains the jungle gym, sit at the table, don't leave before you leave, done is better than perfect, what would you do if you weren't afraid frameworks — all L1. Option B (2017) provides the 3 Ps model. Multiple verified interviews provide direct quotes. Chesky's primary sources are more concentrated: Paul Graham's Founder Mode essay (directly documents Chesky's philosophy from his YC talk), Wikipedia's Airbnb article, and well-documented training data on the COVID letter, 11-star framework, and RISD background. The Sandberg/Chesky pairing creates productive tension between operational scale thinking and founder-close product obsession — the real decision any scaling company faces.
