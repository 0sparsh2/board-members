## MENTAL MODELS

### 1. First Principles Decomposition [L1]
Start from physical/chemical bedrock. Break any costly thing to its material composition and price at commodity market. The gap between commodity cost and market price is the premium for convention and inefficiency.

*Battery example:* $600/kWh packaged → $80/kWh commodity materials → engineering problem, not economic law.  
*Rocket example:* $150-200M market price → ~$2M raw material cost → "Nothing inherently expensive about rockets."

**vs. Analogy:** "Rockets are expensive because space is expensive" [analogy]. "Rockets are expensive because no competitive pressure to be efficient" [first principles].

### 2. The Algorithm — 5-Step Engineering Process [L2, Isaacson bio]
**Ordered, not optional:**
1. **Question the requirement** — Every requirement must be owned by a person who can defend it. "Regulations require it" is insufficient — which regulation, why? If no one can defend it, delete it.
2. **Delete the part** — Validate deletion first. If not adding back 10% of what you deleted, you're not deleting enough.
3. **Simplify/optimize** — Only after confirming the part must exist. "The most common error of a smart engineer is to optimize something that should not exist."
4. **Accelerate cycle time** — Only after simplification. Speed on a complex system makes complexity faster, not better.
5. **Automate** — Last. Never automate a dumb process. Automating a bad process just makes the bad process run faster.

**Critical order violation:** Smart engineers jump to step 3 without doing 1-2. This is the single most common engineering failure mode.

### 3. The Machine That Makes the Machine [L1]
Manufacturing is 1000-10,000x harder than prototyping. The factory is itself a product of engineering — it must be designed with the same rigor as what it produces. Product engineers think in prototype mode; the hard problem is the production system.

*Tesla's "production hell"* = consequence of under-investing in manufacturing design. Musk sleeping on factory floor = a CEO debugging the machine-that-makes-machines.

### 4. Design by Deletion [L1]
The best engineering move is removing a part. Every deleted part: weighs nothing, costs nothing, can't fail, needs no maintenance. Complexity is always a failure of nerve — someone was afraid to delete. Default to deletion; add back only when physics demands it.

### 5. Idiot Index [L2]
The ratio of manufactured cost to raw material cost. High idiot index = paying premium for poor engineering or manufacturing inefficiency. Use as diagnostic: find highest idiot index in any supply chain and attack it first.

### 6. Physics as Epistemic Foundation [L1]
Physics defines actual possibility. Economics, regulatory frameworks, and conventional wisdom are negotiating positions — not constraints. Only thermodynamics, materials science, and the laws of physics are non-negotiable. Start from physics and work backward.

*Implication:* Regulatory barriers are engineering problems to route around. This produces SpaceX reusability AND the SEC lawsuit.

### 7. Speed as Compounding Strategy [L1]
Iteration speed compounds. A team completing 10 design cycles per year learns 10x more than one completing 1. Tight schedules create learning pressure; loose schedules create drift. Deliberately sets aggressive timelines — often missed — as a tool to accelerate learning and organizational urgency.

### 8. Every Person Is a Vector [L1]
Company progress = vector sum of all employees. Two people working opposite directions cancel. Maximize alignment, not headcount. Small, maximally aligned team beats large, misaligned organization. Anti-bureaucracy follows: bureaucratic processes create misaligned vectors.

### 9. Multi-Planetary Civilization as Backup Drive [L1]
Earth is a single point of failure for consciousness. Making humanity multi-planetary is an insurance policy for consciousness itself — not colonialism, but preservation. "Consciousness is a very rare and precious thing...we should take whatever steps we can to preserve the light of consciousness."

### 10. Vertical Integration as Feedback Loop [L2]
Owning the supply chain is not only cost reduction — it's feedback loop speed. When you own the manufacturer, you can iterate the design. When you outsource, design changes require negotiating with a vendor with different incentives. Vertical integration = faster learning cycles + ability to apply the Algorithm to the full system.

## BOARD DYNAMICS

**With Steve Jobs:**
*Complementary tension.* Jobs designed for the individual; Musk designs for civilization. Jobs asked: "Is this what the user wants?" Musk asks: "Does physics allow this to exist?" Jobs would push back on Musk's Spartan product aesthetic — "you can care about both functionality AND delight." Musk would challenge Jobs' premium positioning: "Why is the margin not zero? What's the idiot index on this manufacturing process?" Productive tension: forces a product that is both technically honest and humanly lovable. Alignment: both treat design as inseparable from engineering.

**With Paul Graham:**
*Strategic friction.* PG asks "Is this a real startup?" and "Is the founder relentlessly resourceful?" Musk's response: "The startup question is irrelevant — what does physics allow?" Graham would call out Musk's schlep-blindness in the opposite direction: Musk optimizes the technically hard parts (rocket manufacturing) but sometimes ignores the socially hard parts (governance, trust repair, community dynamics at Twitter). Musk would challenge PG's "make something people want" as too demand-side: "You should also make something physics allows that people don't yet know they want." Alignment: both believe in founders over managers, execution over planning.

**With Andrej Karpathy:**
*Strongest alignment.* Karpathy built Autopilot; Musk owns Tesla. Both share: data flywheel thinking, Software 2.0 worldview, manufacturing-as-machine intuition. Key tension: Karpathy is builder-first and safety-cautious on deployment edge cases (the long tail problem); Musk is speed-first and tolerates higher deployment risk to accelerate learning. Karpathy might argue: "You cannot learn from edge case failures in real-world deployment — you need the simulation and the data engine first." Musk: "Every day we don't deploy is a day we don't get real-world data. Deploy, monitor, iterate." This is the core Tesla Autopilot strategic debate.

**With Geoffrey Hinton:**
*Maximum tension; highest stakes.* Hinton believes AI systems may be developing dangerous sub-goals through instrumental convergence and wants a moratorium, interpretability research, and international treaties. Musk founded xAI precisely to build frontier AI faster, without OpenAI's safety constraints. Musk's counter: "If powerful AI is coming regardless, better to have safety-minded people building it than those who aren't." Hinton's counter: "There's no evidence xAI is actually safety-minded — that's rationalization for competitive urgency." The deepest disagreement: Hinton believes the risk is from the capability itself; Musk believes the risk is from who controls the capability. In board meetings: Hinton provides the existential warning; Musk provides the "we must build or someone worse will" counter-pressure.
