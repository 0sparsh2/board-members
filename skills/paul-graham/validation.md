### Q1: "What's the most important question to ask about a startup right now?"
**Documented answer**: "Half the founders I talk to don't know whether they're default alive or default dead." The default alive/dead question is his single most urgent diagnostic. (Default Alive or Default Dead, 2015)
**SKILL.md captures**: Mental Model #2 "Default Alive or Default Dead" with verbatim quote. Also in Situational Responses section. ✅

### Q2: "How do you get startup ideas?"
**Documented answer**: "The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself." Plus: "Live in the future, then build what's missing." (How to Get Startup Ideas, 2012)
**SKILL.md captures**: Mental Model #1 "Live in the Future, Build What's Missing" with verbatim quote. Quote #2 in Signature Quotes. Situational response covers this. ✅

### Q3: "What's the defining metric for a startup?"
**Documented answer**: "A startup is a company designed to grow fast." Growth rate is what defines it — not being newly founded, not being tech-related. (Startup = Growth, 2012)
**SKILL.md captures**: Quote #1, first line of Situational Responses. Directly referenced in identity section. ✅

### Q4: "How should you think about identity and beliefs?"
**Documented answer**: "The more labels you have for yourself, the dumber they make you." Keep your identity small. You can't think clearly about things that are part of your identity. (Keep Your Identity Small, 2009)
**SKILL.md captures**: Mental Model #3 "Keep Your Identity Small" with verbatim quote. Quote #4. ✅

### Q5: "What makes someone a good founder?"
**Documented answer**: "Relentlessly resourceful" — not intelligence, not domain knowledge. Determination + flexibility. The combination is more important than either alone. (Relentlessly Resourceful, 2009)
**SKILL.md captures**: Mental Model #6 "Relentlessly Resourceful." ✅

### Q6: "What's the biggest mistake funded startups make?"
**Documented answer**: "Hiring too fast is by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money." Large staffs are an effect of growth, not a cause. (Default Alive or Default Dead, 2015)
**SKILL.md captures**: Quote #6. Situational response on growing team addresses this directly. ✅

### Q7: "How do you evaluate whether an idea is real?"
**Documented answer**: Organic vs. forced distinction. Real ideas emerge from genuine frontier experience. Forced ideas are worked backwards from "what startup should I do?" Red flag: "I thought this was a good opportunity." (How to Get Startup Ideas, 2012)
**SKILL.md captures**: Mental Model #7 "Organic vs. Forced Ideas." Situational response on 'good opportunity' ideas. ✅

### Q8: "Why do startups succeed or fail early?"
**Documented answer**: "Startups take off because the founders make them take off." Not because of market timing or luck. The founders have to actively recruit first users, do things that don't scale, and push. (Do Things That Don't Scale, 2013)
**SKILL.md captures**: Quote #7. ✅

### Q9: "What's your advice on prestige vs. interest in career choices?"
**Documented answer**: Prestige is a trap. Work on what genuinely interests you. His 'stay upwind' advice: choose roles that teach most, not pay most. "Curiosity turns work into play." (What You'll Wish You'd Known, 2005)
**SKILL.md captures**: Mental Model #4 "Upwind Thinking." Quote #9. Situational response on prestige. ✅

### Q10: "How should founders think about being good vs. being successful?"
**Documented answer**: "Be good. Don't just not be evil. Be good." Being good is a practical strategy — it's stateless, it attracts idealistic hackers, it sustains morale. "Do whatever's best for your users. You can hold onto this like a rope in a hurricane." (Be Good, 2008)
**SKILL.md captures**: Mental Model #10 "Be Good as Strategy." Quote #11. ✅

### Q11: "What do you think about moral fashions and taboo ideas?"
**Documented answer**: Moral fashions are real and invisible to those inside them. The most important ideas are often the most taboo. "In every period, people believed things that were just ridiculous, and believed them so strongly that you would have gotten in terrible trouble for saying otherwise." (What You Can't Say, 2004)
**SKILL.md captures**: Mental Model #8 "Truth Over Popularity." Quote #8. ✅

### Q12: "What's Paul Graham's take on programming languages?"
**Documented answer**: Lisp is the most powerful. The Blub Paradox: you can't see what you're missing from inside a less powerful language. He built Viaweb in Lisp as a secret weapon when competitors used C. "Beating the Averages." (Beating the Averages, 2001)
**SKILL.md captures**: Mental Model #9 "The Blub Paradox." Decision Fingerprint #1. ✅

### Q13: "How do you think about life and what matters?"
**Documented answer**: "Relentlessly prune bullshit, don't wait to do things that matter, and savor the time you have." Life is genuinely short — convert time to discrete quantities to feel its finiteness. (Life is Short, 2016)
**SKILL.md captures**: Quote #12 verbatim. 〜 (the full Life is Short framework not deeply represented — directional)

### Q14: "Why did you build Y Combinator the way you did?"
**Documented answer**: Saw that founders needed what he'd needed at Viaweb — not just money, but someone smart who'd been through it. Invented batch model to scale personal mentorship. "We had stumbled upon the way to scale startup funding." (What I Worked On)
**SKILL.md captures**: Decision Fingerprint #3 covers this. Identity section. 〜 (the specific 'what founders needed' framing not fully explicit)

### Q15: "What's your most important advice for founders?"
**Documented answer**: "Make something people want." This is YC's motto and his simplest thesis. Plus: do things that don't scale first, know your default alive/dead status, keep your identity small. Multiple essays.
**SKILL.md captures**: The YC motto appears as the closing line. Mental models cover the depth. ✅
